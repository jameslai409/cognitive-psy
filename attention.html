Attention and Consciousness

To be aware of something, you first must bring your attention to it.
Just looking at an object does not mean that your brain is processing it.


Phenomena


  • Convert Misdirection - A gaze on an object does not guarantee awareness of it's state. If the attention is looking elsewhere, the gaze is all for naught.
  • Change Blindness - An interuption between seemingly static images will usually fail to alert the looker of any hidden changes.
  • Inattentional Blindness - This is best depicted by this famous video, which prompts the watcher to count the number of basketball passes while a very strange person in a gorrila costume walks right through the center of the screen. Most people fail to notice the gorilla due to inattentional blindness.
  • Masking - A visual stimulus can "mask" or overwrite a brief prior visual stimulus. Masking is used as a method to measure conscious perception by briefly flashing a word on a screen and quickly masking it with another visual stimulus.

Consciousness

Consciousness is our subjective interpretation of the experiences that come into our awareness, where our attention is looking at. At the neuronal level, simulantaneous neurons fire synchronously, which links activity between different brain regions. Many layers of abstraction layer, this is how we are able to achieve sensory perception and a stream of stimuli.

Other states of consciousness include:

  • Coma
  • Vegetative State
  • Minimally Conscious State
And most importantly:
  • Death - The ultimate lack of consciousness
