Multitasking

Many people like to believe that they are exceptional at juggling multiple tasks at once.
Findings from psychologists all over the world have revealed that task-switching would be a more appropriate word.
The ability to multi-tasking, in fact, comes with drastically reduced performances.


Limitations of the Brain


The brain can only process so many streams of information at once. According to William James, the mind rapidly oscillates between the processes.
This hinders perception, decision-making, and response speed. The working memory system can be overwhelmed and not all information received will be retained.
Recognition memory drops by approximately 50% when working on multiple tasks rather than a single one.

The Solution?

Luckily, the solution is simple.
Don't multitask if you can help it.
Scientifically speaking, the most efficient way to get two tasks done is put 100% of your mind to each task individually, contrary to juggling both at once. Of course, there are exceptions, but switching tasks is usually the way to go.
Even CPUs do it!

Next time, consider just getting those dishes once ASAP and get them out of the way. Or maybe don't try to do homework during lecture. Sometimes, in choosing both, you lose both.
